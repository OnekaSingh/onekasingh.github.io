Foreword:

Abstract:
We present the classical and quantum complexity of linear system problems (LSP), comparing the asymptotic performance of the classical Conjugate Gradient Method (CGM) and the quantum Harrow-Hassidim-Lloyd (HHL) algorithm using Big-O notation. We then lightly explain HHL’s sub-components, with the primary objective of highlighting the quantum algorithm’s response to solving the LSP task, and its corresponding scalability (input size to time).
With an overview of computational complexity classes, we classify CGM and HHL accordingly, and pondering open questions in complexity theory, such as the relationship between P and NP, as well as the position of BQP within PSPACE. We then compare CGM--the fastest classical algorithm for LSP--to the quantum LSP solver, HHL, emphasizing the distinction between their classical and quantum approaches.
Next, we dissect HHL into its core sub-algorithms, including the Inverse Quantum Fourier Transform (IQFT), Quantum Phase Estimation (QPE), and Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT). This section delves into the mathematical principles underpinning each quantum gate, showing the way quantum algorithms like HHL can achieve superior time complexity for higher input sizes. Validating our proof of concept, we implement CGM in Python and analyzed its performance across varying input sizes, referencing Big-O expectations. We then simulate HHL on some of IBM’s Qiskit backends taking in to account input limitations. Conclusively, we discuss current quantum computing architecture limitations which hinders the practicality of quantum algorithms (HHL is better suited on fault-tolerant quantum computers due to hardware restrictions and decoherence).
